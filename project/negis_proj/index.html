Flow history of NEGIS

Aug 1, 2019

The goal

My postdoc in Copenhagen focuses on modeling of the Northeast Greenland Ice Stream (NEGIS). The idea is to do some modeling that takes advantage of/helps interpret the new EastGRIP ice core. NEGIS is a huge system, and a lot of research and modeling has already been done on it, so my goal is to really leverage new, unique information that we can get from having an ice core in the middle of the ice stream. Because the ice core contains records extending back thousands of years, far longer than more direct records of ice flow, so it could give us unique information about the long-term ice flow. The idea that I have started to run with is to use the crystal fabric of the core as a tracer of past flow. For a primer on crystal fabric, take a look at the work I have doing to use fabric as a tracer of past flow.

Past work on NEGIS

When I was still at the University of Washington, I did some work with then-postdoc Nick Holschuh to understand the relationship between radar data collected on NEGIS and the small-scale flow regime. I ran a number of model simulations, which Nick used to aid in interpretation of the radar data and to determine how well the various simulations matched reality. The work suggests that the isochrones do not match the current flow field, but rather imply some transient changes to the ice stream. While Nick was able to obtain some nice results, they give a tantalizing suggestion of instability without allowing us to quantify it or say much about the cause. Consequently, I was extremely excited to get to come to Copenhagen to work on this system as new data are collected.

Anisotropic modeling

For my current work, I’m modeling along flowlines that lead to near the EastGRIP core site. By modeling the development of ice-crystal fabric along multiple flowlines, I can explore a range of possible flow histories (hypothetical ways in which the ice stream might have differed in the past). If we could then associate one these flow histories with what is measured in the ice core, we might then be able to say something about ice flow in the past.

Right now, this work is in its preliminary stages. I have a working model, which took significant time due to all the processes that must be included. One of the many challenges is that many effects must be included; for example, temperature affects how easily ice deforms (see figure below), so we may get a very different fabric if we don’t account for the large change in temperature in Greenland since the last ice age. I’ve worked out these kinks, and am preparing to run model simulations that can more accurately represent fabric formation on these flowlines and thus be compared with ice-core data.

The preliminary results were been encouraging, but the complexity of the different effects led me to take a significant step back and think about the essential aspects of fabric in an ice stream. This led a full project just understanding these effects in the abstract, which is described here.
